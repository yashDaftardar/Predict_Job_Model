Summary

We are looking for a software engineering manager to join our rapidly growing product team. So, if you are an experienced engineering manager who is looking to continue your path while assisting others on their path of continuous learning, continuous improvement, and a team that values outcomes over output… look no further, you may be a Llama!

We are expanding the team, and it is important to us that everyone is supported. One of the most vital ways to ensure everyone is able to do the best work of their lives is to ensure that their management is the best of their careers. This is where you come in.

The technical aspect of this role is incredibly important… however, emotional intelligence and the deep desire to serve are significantly more important. As a Software Engineering Manager, you will have between three and eight direct reports. You will be helping them with their long-term career aspirations, recommending them for promotions and bonuses, holding them accountable, and giving them “in the moment” feedback as well as serving as an active listener during your weekly 1-on-1’s.


How We Work at Lessonly

We are a tight-knit group with diverse backgrounds who value our culture. Individually, we strive to be humble, hungry, and people-smart. As a team we put learners first, we share before we’re ready, we ask clarifying questions, we highlight what’s working, we have difficult conversations, we get agreements, and we make time for life. These aren’t just slogans we put on the wall… these are the values we aspire to live by every day.


Roles & Responsibilities

  • Manager

    • You strive to influence those you serve to achieve their personal goals as well as their work goals via clarity, trust, accountability ( both being accountable to them as well as holding them accountable to our agreements ), empowerment, and being an example of the Lessonly definition of an ideal team player. This plays a major role in the continued success and growth of those we serve.

All 6 of the following examples of success/traits we look for aren’t requirements. They are indications of the type of managers/management-styles we value here at Lessonly.
  • You have strong opinions, weakly held
    • You have an inborn sense of wonder and curiosity that makes you delightful to be around. You don’t judge; you make observations and explore the possibilities. You ask questions and are open to new solutions.
  • You have a calming influence in tough situations
    • With an innate ability to understand what co-workers or clients are going through, you can get through difficult times drama free.
  • You aren’t afraid of change... matter of fact, you embrace adapting
    • You don’t invest valuable time trying to prove you are right when you realize you’re wrong. Instead of looking for excuses, you offer a simple, honest apology that lets you quickly get back on track. It sounds something like this: “I’m sorry I messed up and chose some bad words that sounded like I was attacking you. This is not what I intended. Can I try again?”
  • You have an aptitude for continuous improvement (self-motivated)
    • You balance appreciating what you have and continually striving to be better
    • You have a passion for achieving your goals and are driven not by rewards such as money and prestige, but by accomplishing what you set out to do.
  • You get energy from helping others win
    • Helping those you serve, and seeing them thrive is something that gives you energy and joy.
    • Helping those you serve with their goals, and holding them accountable to their commitments is something you don’t shy away from.
  • You have difficult conversations
    • “We have difficult conversations” is one of Lessonly’s values. It is an incredibly important ambition for our team. When done well, difficult conversations foster psychological safety and give our team a significant competitive advantage. In life and work, there are many valuable conversations that are easier left unsaid. These are the difficult conversations we need to be having—they help us address the tension and conflict that might otherwise keep us from doing better work.

  • Engineering enablement

    • You won't be solely responsible for engineers having the best tools and processes (balancing autonomy and focusing on feedback loops) to do their best work, but you will be heavily relied upon to ensure this is continuously improving.

  • Success looks like:

    • You'll search for the “multiplicative effect”: impact multiplied by the number of people.
    • You'll be proactive by identifying and coordinating how we meet common needs across the engineering team such as standards and conventions, documentation, etc.
    • You'll be reactive by helping coordinate solutions to problems you hear about from the team, such as on-call support being frustrating, or waiting on reviews taking too long.
    • You'll contribute to continually iterating on and improving the Engineer Growth Framework.
